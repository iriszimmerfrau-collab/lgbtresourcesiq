Today is June 1, 2026. For the next thirty days the top of every page on this site carries a small celebratory banner — a brand-coloured stripe and three short messages, one in each of our languages: **You exist. You belong. We are with you.** On July 1 the banner disappears automatically, the way it appeared.

We mark this month because queer Iraqis exist, have always existed, and deserve the same minute of celebration the rest of the world gives itself in June. We mark it knowing exactly what month it is — what the calendar means in our context — and choosing to mark it anyway.

## What we did to the site

- A thin gradient banner at the top of every page during June, in the ISPC palette (magenta → terracotta → saffron → olive → lavender). It scans as the site's own brand, not as a literal pride flag. Dismissable with a × button that persists for the browser session.
- A small "Pride 2026" wordmark in the footer.
- A slight enrichment of the three-rectangle ornament you've already seen above headings — two extra brand colours appended during June.

What we did **not** do:

- No rainbow-flag emojis. No flag glyphs. Nothing that scans as queer in 100 milliseconds of shoulder-surf. The site still has to pass the test of looking like an article from across the room on a shared phone. That test does not relax in June.
- No automatic rebuild on July 1 — the banner is gated by a small browser-side check of the visitor's local clock, so it reverts when their device crosses into July regardless of when the static site is next deployed.

## Why June in Iraq is not the same as June elsewhere

For most of the world, June is when corporate sponsors put rainbows on their logos and the Western media writes its annual "queer joy" essays. For queer Iraqis, June has historically meant:

- **Cleric-led media campaigns** that pick up volume around the international pride news cycle. Sadr-aligned and Iran-aligned figures have used June and July as windows to issue anti-LGBTQ+ statements that drive subsequent enforcement.
- **Militia attention** to queer-coded urban spaces in Baghdad, Basra, Najaf, and Karbala. The 2017 and 2022 documented spikes in entrapment attacks both included a June clustering.
- **Border scrutiny** at Baghdad International and Erbil International stepping up around international LGBT events. Phone searches get longer; the questions get more specific.
- **Family vigilance** spikes when the Iraqi press carries pride-month coverage from abroad. Parents and siblings notice. Conversations get had. Phones get checked.

Knowing all of this, we still choose to mark the month — quietly, in our own colours, with a banner the reader can dismiss in one tap. **Celebration and safety are not opposites.**

## Practical safety adjustments for June

For the next thirty days, consider raising the floor on a few habits:

- **VPN always.** If you've been casual about toggling Mullvad or Proton VPN on and off, leave it on for the month. International pride traffic surges create more correlation opportunities for whoever is watching.
- **Phone hygiene before any border crossing.** Even if you don't normally clean before a trip, do it in June. Re-read the [airport phone-search alert](/en/security-alerts/airport-phone-search-pattern) and treat its checklist as the floor, not the ceiling.
- **Don't post.** Whatever you would post publicly during pride month in a freer country — don't post that thing on any account tied to your real identity. Save the photo to a private encrypted folder. Wait.
- **Watch the news.** [Our security alerts feed](/en/security-alerts) will surface any cleric statement, ministry announcement, or arrest pattern we see during the month. Check it every couple of days.
- **Use Quick Exit.** If you read this site near family, use the ESC key, triple-spacebar, or the Quick Exit button. Practise it once now so it's muscle memory if you need it later.
- **If the banner is too visible** — dismiss it. One tap on the × in its corner hides it for the session. We will not be offended. Reader safety is the higher value.

## What's worth celebrating

The fact that this article exists, in three languages, on a site run by queer Iraqis, that you can actually read inside Iraq, is something queer Iraqis a decade ago would have called impossible. People have done that work — quietly, anonymously, in encrypted side-channels, with cover stories and false names and patience. The collective behind this site is one small node in a longer network of queer Iraqi survival, joy, and care.

If you are reading this from inside Iraq: you exist. You belong. We are with you.

If you are reading this from the diaspora: the people back home need you to keep showing up — by writing, translating, donating, hosting, listening, and refusing to forget. **The work does not pause for the month either.**

We will see each other through.

— The Iraqi Social Progress Collective
